## Abstract
In recent years, the international community has made considerable progress in providing protection for genetic resources and Traditional Knowledge (TK) kept or developed by Indigenous communities. From the Convention on Biological Diversity, the Nagoya Protocol, and the Cartagena Protocol, to the 2024 World Intellectual Property Organization Treaty on Intellectual Property, the international Indigenous community has stronger protection for intellectual property and data sovereignty than ever before. Those protections, however, have not been extended to Tribal Nations and Indigenous communities in the United States. While the United States lags behind the rest of the world in efforts to protect intellectual property for Indigenous communities, it is important that Indigenous communities and Tribal Nations take affirmative steps to protect their intellectual property rights by using the same tools available to any other inventor in the United States through the United States Patent and Trademark Office. This presentation will address the role of the United States patent system in protecting Indigenous intellectual property, including genetic resources and TK. Specifically, this presentation will cover patent subject matter eligibility, with special attention to the eligibility of genetic resources and associated TK for patent protection; the novelty requirement; the non-obviousness requirement; and the requirement for adequate written description and enabling disclosure, in United States patent law. Finally, this presentation will address non-patent tools for protecting genetic resources and associated TK, including licensing agreements and trade secrets.
