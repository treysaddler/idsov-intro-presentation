## Abstract
For millennia, Indigenous Peoples have observed and recorded environmental processes as part of their cultural responsibilities to care for Country. However, Indigenous data, including land, environmental, demographic, social, cultural, linguistic, and oral histories, have largely been collected, stored, and used by non-Indigenous entities without Indigenous governance. As a result, much of the data used in research and modelling about our lands, waters, and skies continues to be generated, held, and applied without Indigenous involvement or authority. Indigenous Data Sovereignty provides a pathway grounded in the inherent rights of self-determination outlined in the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples. In scientific fields such as ecology, climate science, and natural resource management, widely promoted data principles such as the FAIR Principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) are used to enhance data sharing. However, people- and purpose-oriented principles, such as the CARE Principles (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics), remain underutilized. Embedding the CARE Principles presents a critical opportunity for modelers and researchers to ensure that their work respects Indigenous knowledge systems, strengthens partnerships with Indigenous communities, supports fair and equitable benefits for those on whose Country they work, and draws on the intergenerational expertise of Indigenous Peoples in understanding their ecosystems and the data that derive from them. This presentation demonstrates how applying the CARE Principles can strengthen the ethical foundations of scientific modelling, uphold Indigenous rights, and ensure Indigenous communities maintain governance over their data while receiving equitable benefits from research outcomes.
