# (CANCELED) A kaupapa Maori Critique of Maori Food and Nutrition Data in Aotearoa, New Zealand.

## Abstract
Background Maori food and nutrition data collected through national health surveys in Aotearoa New Zealand inform policy decisions, resource allocation, and public health responses. However, these data systems are largely shaped by colonial research paradigms that marginalize Maori knowledge and restrict Maori authority over data concerning their wellbeing. This presentation introduces the concepts of Maori Nutrition Data Sovereignty (MNDS) and Maori Nutrition Data Governance (MNDG), and presents findings from a doctoral research project that developed and applied a new analytical framework to critically assess six major national health surveys containing Maori food and nutrition data. Methods The analysis examined survey governance structures, data collection methods, variable selection, and reporting practices through the lens of MNDS and MNDG principles. A Kaupapa Maori analytical framework was developed, grounded in Maori data sovereignty principles articulated by Te Mana Raraunga. This framework was used to review six national health surveys for alignment with MNDS and MNDG criteria. Publicly available survey documents, methodology reports, and results were analysed to determine the extent to which each survey supported Maori data sovereignty and governance. Results All six national health surveys scored poorly in relation to MNDS and MNDG. The findings highlight significant gaps in culturally grounded and self-determining data practices, with limited Maori decision making authority and persistent use of deficit based narratives in the portrayal of Maori nutrition. These shortcomings contribute to ongoing nutritional health inequities and reinforce colonial data structures.
