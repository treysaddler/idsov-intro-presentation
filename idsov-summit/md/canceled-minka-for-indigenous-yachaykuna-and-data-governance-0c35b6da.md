# (CANCELED) Minka for Indigenous Yachaykuna and Data Governance

## Abstract
This presentation examines how Indigenous researchers are defining, practicing, and transforming data governance through their own protocols, relational responsibilities, and research practices. Grounded in Kichwa values and the principles of IDSov, this paper explores how research practitioners and Kichwa communities assert governance over knowledges and data resisting colonial property frameworks that continue to shape global research systems. Drawing from semi-structured interviews with 32 researchers across the globe, the study follows the Kichwa principle of minka—collective labor and shared responsibility—to understand governance of data as an ethical, relational, and community-based practice rather than an institutional mechanism. Furthermore, this work acknowledges that Indigenous methodologies alone have not fully resolved the asymmetries and privileges embedded in academic research or the tensions between institutional intellectual property regimes and Indigenous knowledge sovereignty. As an Indigenous researcher working within these constraints, I pause to face rather than obscure these methodological conundrums, seeing in them opportunities to articulate and assert the protection of Indigenous knowledges through relationships between ideas and practices. Through three interwoven clusters—refusals, principles, and practices—the study highlights how Indigenous practitioners resist extractive research norms, enact sovereignty-based frameworks, and sustain accountability through agreements, collective authorship, and relational care. These practices illuminate Indigenous data governance as both the assertion of rights over data, and the continual weaving of responsibility, reciprocity, and protection across all stages of the data life cycle.
